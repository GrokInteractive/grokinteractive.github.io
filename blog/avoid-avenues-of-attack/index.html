Avoid obvious avenues of attack

by Jason Ellis on Mar 05, 2014 Software development Security

This may seem like a no-brainer; When creating a new web application you should be conscious of and avoid obvious avenues of attack.

I said it was a no-brainer, but you would be shocked at how often I see sites created by professional developers who take things for granted and end up yielding information to dataminers and attackers who know what to look for.

One example mistake that is often made are responsive error messages that give too much information.

On the one hand it seems like you are doing your users a favor by telling them that when they attempted to login, the system found the email address but the password was wrong or that the email they entered did not exist.

Consider what you are telling the user when you respond that the email was found but the password was wrong. You are saying, "Hey.. that's a valid email we have in our system." Think of all the ways that can be abused. How hard would it be to create a bot that bombards a login prompt with a known list of emails (which are trivial to get btw) to suddenly find usage patterns or even simple email validation.

The response for a valid email and an invalid email, valid password or invalid password should always be the same. It should be enough information to tell a user that the login failed, without divulging the reason.

Another often abused and mined avenue of attack is server routes. If an unauthorized user tries to go to a route on your server that is completely invalid, or they try to go to a valid route they aren't authorized to go to, the response should be the same.

You can find the administration area for many sites by just typing in /admin as an unauthorized user and seeing a completely different set of pages and responses than when you try to go to /foobar.

Be consistent. Consider what information you are leaking by having inconsistent or overly verbose error messages. Be mindful that each nugget of information, while likely useless on it's own, helps the clever attacker build a bigger picture.

I am not a proponent of security through obscurity, but every layer helps. Think about it.
